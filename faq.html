FAQs

Periodontitis is a type of gum disease that develops as a result of the accumulation of plaque and bacteria on the teeth and gums. Plaque is a sticky film that forms on the teeth when food particles mix with bacteria in the mouth. Over time, if plaque is not removed through regular brushing and flossing, it can harden into calculus. Calculus is much harder to remove than plaque and can only be removed by a professional dental cleaning.

When plaque and calculus build up on the teeth and gums, the bacteria begin to release toxins that irritate the gums and cause inflammation. This inflammation is called gingivitis, which is the earliest stage of gum disease. If left untreated, the inflammation can spread to the bone and other supporting tissues that hold the teeth in place, leading to periodontitis.

The symptoms of periodontitis may include red, swollen, and bleeding gums, bad breath, receding gums, loose teeth, and tooth loss. If you experience any of these symptoms, it is important to seek treatment from a periodontist or dental professional.

Periodontitis can be treated in several ways, depending on the severity of the disease. In the early stages, non-surgical treatments such as scaling and root debridement, also known as a deep cleaning, may be effective in removing plaque and bacteria from the teeth and gums. Antibiotics may also be prescribed to control infection and reduce inflammation.

In more severe cases, surgical treatment may be necessary. This may involve flap surgery, which involves lifting the gums to clean the roots of the teeth and remove tartar and bacteria, and bone grafting, which involves replacing lost bone tissue to support the teeth. In extreme cases, tooth extraction may be necessary.

To prevent periodontitis from developing or progressing, it is important to maintain good oral hygiene practices, such as brushing twice daily, flossing daily, and attending regular dental check-ups and cleanings. Additionally, avoid smoking or using tobacco products, eat a healthy diet, and manage any underlying health conditions that may affect your periodontal health.

Periodontitis and general health:

Research has linked periodontitis to several systemic health conditions, including:

Heart disease: Studies have found that people with periodontitis have an increased risk of heart disease, likely due to the inflammation and bacteria associated with gum disease.

Diabetes: Diabetes and periodontitis are closely linked, with research suggesting that people with diabetes are more likely to develop gum disease and that treating periodontitis can help improve blood sugar control.

Respiratory infections: The bacteria associated with periodontitis can be inhaled into the lungs and potentially contribute to respiratory infections.

Pregnancy complications: women with periodontitis may be at an increased risk of premature birth and low birth weight babies.

Alzheimer's disease: There is some evidence to suggest that the bacteria associated with periodontitis may contribute to the development of Alzheimer's disease.

Overall, while the exact mechanisms behind the relationship between periodontitis and systemic health conditions are still being studied, it is clear that good oral hygiene and regular dental check-ups are important for both oral and overall health. If you have periodontitis, your dentist or periodontist can provide personalized recommendations for managing your condition and reducing your risk of associated health problems.

It is important to visit a periodontist for a consultation prior to treatment for several reasons:

Accurate diagnosis: periodontist is a specialist who has received advanced training and education in the diagnosis and treatment of periodontal disease and other related conditions. A consultation with a periodontist can ensure that your condition is accurately diagnosed, and the appropriate treatment plan is recommended.

Treatment options: A periodontist can discuss the various treatment options available for your specific condition and provide you with information about the pros and cons of each treatment option. This can help you make an informed decision about the best course of action for your individual needs.

Comprehensive care: periodontist can provide you with a comprehensive treatment plan that takes into account your overall oral health and any underlying medical conditions that may affect your periodontal health. This can help ensure that you receive the best possible care and that your treatment is tailored to your individual needs.

Long-term management: A periodontist can provide you with advice and guidance on how to manage your condition over the long term, including tips for maintaining good oral hygiene practices, dietary recommendations, and lifestyle changes that can help improve your overall periodontal health.

In summary, a consultation with a periodontist prior to treatment can help ensure that you receive an accurate diagnosis, the best possible treatment options, and comprehensive care tailored to your individual needs, as well as advice on long-term management of your condition.

Periodontitis is a complex disease that has a multifactorial background. Several factors can contribute to the development and progression of periodontitis, including:

Poor oral hygiene: inadequate brushing and flossing can lead to the accumulation of plaque and tartar on the teeth, which can contribute to the development of periodontitis.

Genetics: research has shown that some people may be more susceptible to periodontitis due to genetic factors.

Smoking: smoking is a major risk factor for periodontitis. It can affect the body's immune response and increase the risk of infection and inflammation.

Diabetes: people with diabetes are at increased risk of developing periodontitis due to their impaired immune response and increased susceptibility to infection.

Hormonal changes: hormonal changes during puberty, pregnancy, and menopause can increase the risk of developing periodontitis.

Medications: medications, such as immunosuppressants and anticonvulsants, can affect the body's immune response and increase the risk of periodontitis.

Stress: stress can affect the body's immune response and increase the risk of inflammation and infection.

Nutritional deficiencies: a diet lacking in essential nutrients, such as vitamin C, can affect the body's ability to fight infection and inflammation.

Treatment of periodontitis typically involves a combination of professional cleaning, antibiotics, and in severe cases, surgical intervention. However, prevention through good oral hygiene practices and addressing risk factors, such as smoking and diabetes, is the best way to avoid developing periodontitis. Regular dental check-ups and cleanings are also important in the early detection and management of periodontal disease.

Changes in age, general health, and medications taken can affect dental implants in several ways:

Age: we age, bone density can decrease, which may affect the success of dental implants. Older patients may also be more susceptible to gum disease, which can also impact implant success. However, with proper planning and careful assessment, dental implants can still be a viable option for older patients.

General health: Patients with certain medical conditions, such as uncontrolled diabetes or autoimmune diseases, may have a higher risk of implant failure. Poor general health can also affect the body's ability to heal after implant surgery, which can impact implant success. It is important for patients to disclose their medical history to their implant surgeon to ensure they are good candidates for the procedure.

Medications: Certain medications, such as bisphosphonates, used to treat osteoporosis, can affect bone density and may increase the risk of implant failure. Other medications, such as blood thinners, may increase the risk of bleeding during implant surgery. It is important for patients to disclose all medications they are taking to their implant surgeon to ensure the procedure can be safely performed.

In summary, changes in age, general health, and medications taken can affect dental implants in various ways, but with proper planning and careful assessment, dental implants can still be a viable option for many patients. It is important for patients to disclose their medical history and medications to their implant surgeon to ensure the procedure can be safely performed.

Peri-implantitis is a type of gum disease that affects dental implants. It is characterized by inflammation and infection of the soft and hard tissues surrounding the dental implant. Similar to periodontal disease, peri-implantitis is caused by bacteria that accumulate around the implant and cause an inflammatory response. If left untreated, peri-implantitis can lead to bone loss and implant failure.

Some of the signs and symptoms of peri-implantitis include:

  • Bleeding gums around the implant
  • Swelling and inflammation of the gums
  • Receding gums
  • Pus or discharge around the implant
  • Pain or tenderness around the implant
  • Loose or wobbly implant

The risk of developing peri-implantitis can be minimized by maintaining good oral hygiene practices and attending regular dental check-ups. Treatment for peri-implantitis may involve non-surgical or surgical interventions, such as scaling and root debridement, antibiotics, or implant removal in severe cases. It is important to consult with a periodontist or dental implant specialist if you suspect that you may have peri-implantitis.

Your general health and the medications you take can have a significant impact on the development and progression of peri-implantitis, which is a form of inflammation and infection that affects the tissues surrounding dental implants. Here are some ways in which general health and medication can affect peri-implantitis:

Systemic health conditions: Certain systemic health conditions, such as diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and osteoporosis, can increase the risk of developing peri-implantitis. These conditions can affect the immune system and the ability of the body to heal, making it more difficult to manage inflammation and infection around the implant.

Medications: Some medications, such as immunosuppressants and bisphosphonates, can also affect the immune system and bone metabolism, which can increase the risk of peri-implantitis. In addition, some medications can cause dry mouth, which can lead to a greater risk of bacterial colonization and infection around the implant.

Smoking: is a significant risk factor for peri-implantitis, as it can affect the immune system and reduce blood flow to the tissues surrounding the implant, making it more difficult for the body to fight infection and inflammation.

Oral hygiene: good oral hygiene is important in preventing peri-implantitis, and certain medications can affect the oral environment and make it more difficult to maintain good oral hygiene. For example, medications that cause dry mouth can lead to a greater accumulation of bacteria and plaque around the implant.

Nutrition: Good nutrition is important for overall health and can also affect the health of the tissues surrounding the implant. Adequate intake of vitamins and minerals, such as vitamin D and calcium, is important for bone health, which is critical in supporting the implant.

In summary, your general health and the medications you take can have a significant impact on the development and progression of peri-implantitis. It is important to inform your dental professional about any health conditions or medications you are taking, as this can help them to develop an appropriate treatment plan and monitor your oral health more closely.

Bone grafting is a surgical procedure used to replace or augment bone that has been lost or damaged due to injury, disease, or trauma. In the context of dental implant placement, bone grafting is often necessary to provide a stable and supportive base for the implant to be placed.

A periodontist is a dental specialist who has additional training and experience in the treatment of the gums, bones, and other tissues that support the teeth. When it comes to bone grafting prior to dental implant placement, there are several reasons why it is important to have a specialist periodontist complete the procedure:

Expertise: periodontist has specialized knowledge and experience in the treatment of the supporting structures of the teeth, including bone grafting. This expertise can help ensure that the procedure is performed correctly and with the highest level of precision.

Customized treatment: Every patient's needs are unique, and a periodontist can evaluate the specific requirements of each patient and develop a customized treatment plan that is tailored to their individual needs.

Advanced techniques: periodontist has access to advanced techniques and technologies that can improve the outcomes of bone grafting procedures. For example, a periodontist may use guided bone regeneration techniques to help stimulate new bone growth in areas where bone density is low.

Reduced risk of complications: grafting procedures can be complex, and there is a risk of complications if they are not performed correctly. Having a periodontist complete the procedure can help reduce this risk, as they have the expertise and experience to manage any potential complications.

Overall, having a specialist periodontist complete your bone grafts prior to dental implant placement can help ensure the best possible outcomes and a successful dental implant procedure.

Keratinized mucosal tissue is an important component of the soft tissue around dental implants. This tissue is characterized by a thick, tough outer layer of keratinized epithelial cells, and a layer of connective tissue that attaches the mucosa to the underlying bone.

There are several reasons why keratinized mucosal tissue is important around dental implants:

Protection: thick, tough outer layer of keratinized epithelial cells helps protect the underlying soft tissue and bone from damage due to mechanical or bacterial trauma.

Stability: mucosal tissue helps provide stability and support for dental implants, which can help prevent implant failure and improve long-term outcomes.

Esthetics: mucosal tissue can help provide a more esthetically pleasing appearance around dental implants by providing a natural-looking transition from the implant to the surrounding soft tissue.

Comfort: with a sufficient amount of keratinized mucosal tissue around their dental implants may experience increased comfort and reduced sensitivity due to the protective nature of the tissue.

For these reasons, it is important for dental professionals to carefully evaluate and manage the amount and quality of keratinized mucosal tissue around dental implants to optimize implant success and patient outcomes.

The lifespan of a dental implant can vary depending on a number of factors, such as the patient's overall health, oral hygiene practices, the quality and quantity of the surrounding bone, the implant material, and the experience of the implant surgeon.

While there is no guarantee on how long a dental implant will last, research suggests that dental implants can last for many years with proper care and maintenance. In fact, studies have reported success rates of over 95% for dental implants after 5 years, and over 90% after 10 years.

Many dental implant manufacturers offer warranties ranging from 5 to 10 years, and some offer lifetime warranties, although these warranties typically only cover the implant fixture itself and not the abutment or restoration.

With proper care and maintenance, a dental implant can last for many years, and even a lifetime in some cases. This includes regular dental checkups, proper oral hygiene practices, and avoiding habits such as smoking, which can increase the risk of implant failure.

Diet

There is some evidence to suggest that a low carbohydrate diet may be linked to the management of periodontal disease. Periodontal disease is a chronic inflammatory condition that affects the gums and bone that support the teeth. It is caused by bacteria in dental plaque, and the body's immune response to the bacteria can lead to tissue damage and bone loss.

Research has shown that a low carbohydrate diet may help reduce inflammation in the body, which could potentially have a positive effect on periodontal disease. This is because carbohydrates can stimulate the production of inflammatory cytokines, which can exacerbate the inflammatory response in the body.

A study published in the Journal of Clinical Periodontology in 2013 found that patients with periodontal disease who followed a low carbohydrate diet for 12 weeks experienced a significant reduction in gum bleeding, pocket depth, and plaque levels compared to those who followed a control diet. The low carbohydrate diet was also associated with a decrease in systemic inflammatory markers.

Another study published in the Journal of Dental Research in 2018 found that a low carbohydrate diet was associated with a decrease in periodontal pathogens in the saliva of overweight and obese individuals.

However, it is important to note that more research is needed to fully understand the relationship between a low carbohydrate diet and periodontal disease management. A healthy diet that is rich in whole foods, including fruits, vegetables, lean protein sources, and healthy fats, along with regular dental care, is still the most effective way to prevent and manage periodontal disease.

Vitamin D

There is some evidence to suggest that there may be a correlation between Vitamin D levels and dental implant failure. Vitamin D is an essential nutrient that plays an important role in bone health, and inadequate levels of Vitamin D can result in a weakened bone structure.

A study published in the Journal of Oral Implantology in 2018 found that patients with lower levels of Vitamin D were more likely to experience implant failure. The study looked at 1,500 dental implants placed in 700 patients, and found that those with lower levels of Vitamin D had a significantly higher risk of implant failure.

Other studies have also suggested a link between Vitamin D levels and dental implant success. However, it is important to note that the evidence is not yet conclusive, and more research is needed to fully understand the relationship between Vitamin D and dental implant success.

In any case, maintaining adequate levels of Vitamin D through a healthy diet, exposure to sunlight, and supplementation as needed may help support overall bone health and potentially improve the success of dental implant procedures.

Dental malocclusion and cranio-mandibular disorders (CMD) can impact the development and treatment of periodontitis in several ways:

Occlusal Trauma: malocclusion can cause occlusal trauma, which occurs when excessive or improperly distributed forces are placed on teeth during chewing or biting. This can lead to damage to the periodontal tissues, including the gums and supporting bone. Over time, occlusal trauma can contribute to the development of periodontitis.

Occlusal Interferences: interferences occur when teeth do not come together properly when the jaws are closed. This can lead to excessive force on certain teeth, which can cause trauma to the periodontal tissues and contribute to the development of periodontitis.

Muscle Tension: can cause muscle tension in the jaw, neck, and shoulder area, which can lead to clenching or grinding of the teeth (bruxism) . Bruxism can cause trauma to the periodontal tissues and lead to the development of periodontitis.

Difficulty with Oral Hygiene: malocclusion and CMD can make it difficult to maintain good oral hygiene. For example, if teeth are crowded or misaligned, it can be difficult to clean between them properly. This can lead to the buildup of plaque and bacteria, which can contribute to the development of periodontitis.

Treatment Considerations: When treating periodontitis in patients with dental malocclusion or CMD, it may be necessary to address these underlying conditions in order to achieve a successful outcome. This may include orthodontic treatment to correct malocclusion or the use of occlusal appliances to manage bruxism.

In summary, dental malocclusion and cranio-mandibular disorders can impact the development and treatment of periodontitis through a variety of mechanisms. It is important for dental professionals to take these factors into consideration when developing a treatment plan for patients with periodontitis.
